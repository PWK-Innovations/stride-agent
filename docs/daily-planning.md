# Daily Planning

Daily planning is the practice of spending a short session at the start of each day to decide what you will work on, in what order, and for how long. It bridges your larger goals and the actual hours of your day. Without a daily plan, you default to reacting to whatever feels most urgent, which rarely aligns with what matters most. A consistent daily planning habit is one of the highest-leverage productivity practices because it compounds: each well-planned day builds momentum for the next.

## The Morning Review Routine

The daily planning session works best as a structured ritual lasting five to ten minutes, performed first thing in the morning before you open email or respond to any external input. The routine follows a simple sequence:

1. **Review your calendar.** Check what meetings, appointments, and fixed commitments you have today. These are immovable blocks that constrain your available time.
2. **Review your task list.** Look at everything that is pending: carryover items from yesterday, tasks from your weekly plan, newly captured items, and any delegated work that needs follow-up.
3. **Identify your top three priorities.** From everything on your plate, select the three tasks that would make today a success if they were the only things you accomplished. These are your MITs, or Most Important Tasks.
4. **Estimate time for each task.** Assign a rough duration to your MITs and any other tasks you plan to tackle. This forces realistic thinking about what actually fits in the day.
5. **Assign tasks to time blocks.** Place your MITs into specific blocks on your schedule, starting with your highest-energy window. Fill remaining blocks with secondary tasks, shallow work, and buffer time.

The entire process should fit on a single page or a single screen. Resist the urge to over-plan. The goal is clarity about priorities and a realistic structure for the day, not a minute-by-minute script.

## Most Important Tasks (MITs)

Your MITs are the tasks that, if completed, would create the most progress, eliminate the biggest bottleneck, or generate the most value. They are not necessarily the most urgent tasks. Selecting three MITs each morning forces you to confront what actually matters rather than defaulting to what is easiest or loudest.

Place your MITs early in the day, before meetings and interruptions erode your energy. If you accomplish nothing else beyond your three MITs, the day should still feel like a success.

## Time Estimation and the Planning Fallacy

The planning fallacy, coined by Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky, describes the tendency to underestimate the time required for future actions. In daily planning, this manifests as scheduling six hours of tasks into four hours of available time.

To counteract the planning fallacy:

- **Use past data.** If you tracked how long similar tasks took previously, use that data rather than your intuition.
- **Add a buffer multiplier.** Multiply your initial estimate by 1.5. If you think a task will take one hour, block 90 minutes.
- **Plan for 60 to 70 percent of your available time.** If you have eight hours in a workday, plan five to six hours of focused tasks. The rest will be consumed by transitions, interruptions, and breaks.

## Structuring Your Day Around Energy

Your cognitive capacity fluctuates throughout the day. Most people experience peak focus in the morning, a dip after lunch, and a modest recovery in the late afternoon. Effective daily planning respects this rhythm:

- **Deep work blocks in the morning.** Schedule your most demanding tasks (writing, coding, strategic thinking, creative work) during your first two to four hours. Protect this window from meetings and shallow tasks.
- **Administrative and collaborative work in the midday.** Meetings, email, messages, and routine tasks are better suited to the post-lunch period when deep focus is harder to sustain.
- **Light tasks and review in the late afternoon.** Use the end of the day for planning, organizing, low-stakes communication, and wrapping up loose ends.

If your peak energy falls at a different time, adjust accordingly. The principle is the same: match task difficulty to energy availability.

## The End-of-Day Review

Spend five to ten minutes before closing out your workday answering three questions:

1. **What did I accomplish today?** Check off completed tasks and acknowledge progress. This creates a sense of closure and prevents the feeling that you worked all day but achieved nothing.
2. **What did not get done, and why?** Identify any tasks that rolled over. Were your estimates too optimistic? Did unexpected interruptions consume your time? Did you procrastinate on something? Understanding why helps you plan better tomorrow.
3. **What is the most important thing to start with tomorrow?** Capture at least one MIT for the next day while today's context is fresh. This gives you a running start in the morning.

## The Shutdown Ritual

Cal Newport recommends a formal shutdown ritual to mark the boundary between work and personal time. The ritual involves reviewing your task list, confirming that every open item has a plan or a place (either scheduled for a specific day or captured in a trusted system), and then saying a specific shutdown phrase (Newport uses "shutdown complete"). This deliberate closure allows your mind to disengage from work, which improves rest quality and prevents the low-grade anxiety of unfinished tasks lingering in the background.

## Connection to Weekly Planning

Daily planning is most effective when nested within a weekly planning practice. Each week, spend 20 to 30 minutes reviewing your goals, projects, and commitments to identify the key outcomes for the week. Your daily planning sessions then draw from this weekly plan, ensuring that each day contributes to larger objectives rather than drifting in response to daily noise.

## Tools and Templates

The best daily planning system is one you will use consistently. What matters is that your system supports three things: a place to list tasks, a way to assign them to time blocks, and a mechanism for review.
